Overview of the Presidential Election Process

The election process starts in the spring of the year before an election. Candidates will register with the Federal Election Comission to run and announce their intentions to run. Then, primary and caucus debates take place. Following this, States and parties hold presidential primaries and caucuses. After that, parties will hold nominating conventions to choose their candidates. Then, candidates will participate in presidential debates. Finally, election day is in early November of election year.

Requirements for Presidential Candidates

(1) Be a natural-born citizen of the United States, (2) Be at least 35 years old, (3) Have been a resident of the United States for 14 years

Primaries, Caucuses, and National Conventions

6-9 months before a presidential election, most states will host primaries. Here, primary voters anonymously choose their preferred canddiate. Several states hold caucuses, which are meetings run by political parties held at the county, district, or precinct level. How these states choose their candidates vary, some by secret ballot, others by group. Once delegates are selected by either method, they go on to represent their state at the national convention, for their respective party. At the national convention, a delegate gets voted to become their party's presidential nominee. This is also where the nominee will announce their running mate, who would become the Vice President-elect upon winning the election.

General Election and the Electoral College

Here, citizens will vote on who they would like to be president. But, it's actually the electoral college who decides which candidate becomes president. This is an old tradition set by the United States in 1787. Each state has a set number of electoral votes based on population, which you can see in the interactive map on your right. Most states' electors will vote based on whoever wins the majority in their state. This means if a candidate wins the majority of California for example, California will cast their electoral votes for that candidate. Whoever has the most electoral college votes at the end then becomes President-elect.
